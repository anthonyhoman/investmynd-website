Nature & Nurture: What Drives Your Financial Decisions?
March 2016 by Andrea Bornstein Bayer
Your financial habits have as much to do with nature - what’s in your DNA, as nurture - your life experiences.

Understanding your investment personality, including how you think and feel about managing your wealth, provides insight into the forces that affect your investing behavior. Pioneer in behavioral finance, Professor Hersh Shefrin, contends that this knowledge, used wisely, can help you become a smarter and potentially more confident investor and may help you avoid repeating costly mistakes.

Investing confidence is heavily influenced by your personal investment knowledge and experiences, as well as your age and gender. In general, humans have a bias towards being confident. From an evolutionary perspective, this confidence has helped us survive and assisted us in succeeding. Indeed, without confidence, we would not take many of the risks required to live our everyday lives.

While being confident is generally helpful, being overconfident can lead to taking too much risk when it comes to investing. Over confidence can allow us to lose sight of the facts and base decisions solely on positive past experiences, taking into consideration only information that supports our investing decisions and potentially ignoring risk.

Individuals with lower confidence can be overly conservative, not taking the appropriate risks required to meet their financial goals. They also tend to procrastinate when it comes to making financial decisions. As a result, they can miss out on opportunities, with the consequence being lower portfolio returns over the long run.

There are both pros and cons to having high or low confidence. Knowing where you fall on the confidence scale can help you understand and avoid typical behavioral pitfalls.

Demystifying Behavioral Terminology - Anchoring
February 2016 by Andrea Bornstein Bayer
Have you ever missed the boat on an investment because you were anchored to an unrealistic price?

The fields of Behavioral Finance and Investments are filled with jargon and acronyms, and we as professionals love to use terms that make us sound highly knowledgeable. I frequently find myself in conversations with colleagues that are not much more than a conscious stream of buzz words intended to impress each other. This behavior might impress fellow advisors and academics, but it can be a real turn-off for wealth management clients.

What is important is that individuals understand the concepts behind the terms and the potential impact on their financial decisions. In this series on Behavioral Finance Concepts and Terminology we will attempt to demystify many of the commonly used terms and provide insight into how one can hopefully avoid typical behavioral pitfalls.

We’ll start with Anchoring – the tendency to lock in on a reference point that may not be relevant to the decision at hand. Consider the investor who bought a stock immediately after the IPO just as the stock price soared. In the next few months the price dropped to a level below the IPO price and then experienced only moderate growth for the next few years, never reaching the investor’s purchase price. The investor should sell this non-performing asset, but is instead waiting for it to get back to the IPO price or higher – the “anchor” price.

Unfortunately, the post IPO surge was driven by market buzz and not company fundamentals. As a result of not taking into consideration the current fundamentals and instead locking onto an irrelevant IPO price the investor is missing the opportunity to invest in better performing assets and potentially passing up the opportunity to offset a capital gain elsewhere in their portfolio. This anchoring has resulted in a both an investment opportunity cost and a real tax cost.

According to Professor of Behavioral Finance, Hersh Shefrin, to avoid the potential pitfalls associated with Anchoring it is important to consider current and relevant information from multiple sources, focus on critical thinking, and let go of emotional attachments to asset prices.

The best investment advisors are able to work closely with their clients to understand their reasons for anchoring and to provide guidance to help them see things a little more realistically. Unfortunately, it often takes a minor crisis to release anchors that have settled in deep. When considering your investments, give your lines a little tug now and then to make sure you can set sail when needed.

Why Behavior Matters in Wealth Management
January 2016 by Andrea Bornstein Bayer

Research shows that investors are more likely to fire an advisor for not understanding their needs than for poor portfolio performance. Taking the time to understand what is really driving an investor will increase client retention far more than slick reports or promises of better performance.

Behavioral Finance looks at how key personality traits can influence an individual’s biases and social/emotional profile. Social/emotional needs related to wealth management can span a wide spectrum such as the need to be in control of all decisions no matter how minor, desire for social inclusion in exclusive investment opportunities, the need to feel highly informed, or the desire to be relieved of responsibility. A client with a high need for control will require a different type of engagement than one with a desire to pass on responsibility.

In addition to identifying one’s social/emotional needs, understanding the underlying personality traits can provide information critical to managing a successful relationship. According to Professor of Behavioral Finance, Hersh Shefrin, understanding an investor’s behavioral traits and biases can shed light on how they may respond to stressful financial situations. Knowing how to manage relationships with different personality types can help avoid misunderstandings that could potentially end an advisory relationship.

We all know that markets go up and markets go down. How someone responds to portfolio movements depends on their perception of how a specific incident will impact their ability to meet their goals as well as their general attitude in life. For example, highly optimistic and agreeable individuals may not even pay attention to downturns, assuming everything will eventually turn out for the best. While this may make life seem a little more pleasant, it can result in ignoring signs that it is time to make critical adjustments to their portfolio.

On the opposite end of the spectrum, less optimistic or less agreeable individuals may panic at every downtick and constantly blame their advisor for making the wrong recommendations. These individuals may never be happy with the message, so how the message is delivered is of great importance. Managing this type of relationship may require a lot of hand holdng and providing a significant amount of information on a very regular basis.

Books and Articles by Hersh Shefrin
"Beyond Greed and Fear: Understanding Behavioral Finance and the Psychology of Investing"

Through colorful, often humorous real-world examples, Shefrin points out the common but costly mistakes that money managers, security analysts, financial planners, investment bankers, and corporate leaders make, so that readers gain valuable insights into their own financial decisions and those of their employees, asset managers, and advisors.
"Behavioralizing Finance"

Behavioralizing Finance provides a structured approach to behavioral finance in respect to underlying psychological concepts, formal framework, testable hypotheses, and empirical findings. A key theme of the volume is that the future of finance will combine realistic assumptions from behavioral finance and rigorous analysis from neoclassical finance.
"A Behavioral Approach to Asset Pricing"

Incorporating the latest research and theory, Shefrin offers both a strong theory and efficient empirical tools that address derivatives, fixed income securities, mean-variance efficient portfolios, and the market portfolio.
"Ending the Management Illusion: How to Drive Business Results Using the Principles of Behavioral Finance"

In Ending the Management Illusion, behavioral finance pioneer Hersh Shefrin addresses the biases that can take you or your organization off course and shows how to run psychologically smart businesses-specifically as it affects your bottom line.
